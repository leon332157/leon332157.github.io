Migrant Workers On Strike

By Dave
The migrant workers from the 1900’s to the 1930’s where creating unions and organizations to go on strike so they could get a better wage and better working conditions. During this time migrant workers displaced form Arkansas, Texas and Oklahoma made groups to go on strike. Also, western businessmen searched all over Asia and Latin America, and thousands of people got recruited to come to California farms to work. Most Asian workers were blamed for taking Americans jobs so they made racist laws to prevent them from working there. Laws were passed forbidding Filipino women from entering the U.S. Also in many rural towns it was a crime for Filipino men to associate with women of other races. \ In 1934, Filipino lettuce cutters and mainly white packing shed workers (AFL) struck the powerful Salinas Valley grower shippers, demanding union recognition and improved conditions. After several weeks on strike the packers started to organize vigilante gangs who burned down the Filipinos labor camps and drove them out of the valley. Packingshed union leaders forgot their agreement with the Filipino field workers, and they hired a vigilante army of 3000, using police and sheriffs to arrest and beat workers. During 1933 cotton strike wages had dropped from $1 to 40 cents per 100 pounds of cotton picked, and the CAWIU, one of the biggest migrant worker unions called for a strike with 1,800 people in it, three fourths of which were Mexican. Things got out of hand and the growers then called on the police and formed armed vigilante squads, which shot down 11 strikers, killing two. After a 24 day strike the wages for the farmers went up to 75 cents per 100 pounds picked. After the cotton strike, the biggest named farm corporations wanted a comeback so they created the Farmers Association which pressured courts and police to arrest CAWIU leaders under the charge of criminal syndicalism (union organizing). By the end of 1934, the CAWIU was gone forever.
